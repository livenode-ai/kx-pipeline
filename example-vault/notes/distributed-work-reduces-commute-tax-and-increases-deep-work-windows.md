# Distributed work reduces commute tax and increases deep work windows

## 主張（Claim）

Distributed work is most valuable when it is treated as a scheduling architecture for cognitive states, not as a perk or a trust policy. Remote-first days reduce commute tax and create earlier, cleaner deep work windows, while office days should be reserved for collaboration that genuinely benefits from shared presence.

## 根拠（Evidence）

The raw capture describes a commute that consumes time before work starts and also delays mental readiness after arrival. By contrast, remote days create a protected morning block before communication channels become noisy.

The useful distinction is not "remote is better than office." The office has clear value for ambiguous alignment, onboarding, mood-reading, and fast social repair. The stronger claim is that different work modes require different cognitive conditions. Deep production benefits from quiet continuity; messy coordination benefits from high-bandwidth interaction.

## データ（Data）

- Example commute day: left at 7:40 a.m., arrived at 9:05 a.m., opened the laptop around 9:25 a.m., and began focused thinking near 10:00 a.m.
- Example remote day: produced one clean 90-minute focus block before Slack became noisy.

## 制約（Constraints）

This claim applies best to knowledge work where output depends on sustained attention and where collaboration can be intentionally scheduled. It does not imply that fully remote work is always superior, or that physical presence has no role. Teams with heavy onboarding, crisis response, equipment constraints, or strong informal coordination needs may require more office time.
